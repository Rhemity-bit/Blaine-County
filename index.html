Internal Documentation
Standard Operating Procedures
San Andreas Highway Patrol  ·  All Personnel Required
REV. OCT 2026

These Standard Operating Procedures establish a comprehensive framework for the San Andreas Highway Patrol Division, designed to ensure safety, efficiency, and consistency in all aspects of operations. Covering routine patrols, traffic enforcement, emergency responses, and specialised highway safety tasks, the SOPs provide clear protocols to assist personnel in their daily responsibilities and decision-making processes.

Compliance with these procedures is mandatory for all members of the Highway Patrol Division. Any exceptions must be formally approved by command staff. Written by Phil Graves, Dan Murphy & Bing Lei — 2026 Edition.

§1 Definitions & Abbreviations ▾

SASP: San Andreas State Police — the principal law enforcement agency responsible for public safety and order within its jurisdiction, including the Highway Patrol division.

HIGHWAY PATROL: The division within SASP tasked with enforcing traffic laws and ensuring road safety on all roads within the state. Handles patrols, traffic management, accident response, and enforcement operations.

PURSUIT: The act of actively following a vehicle attempting to evade law enforcement, involving high-speed driving and tactical efforts to apprehend while minimising risk.

MDT (Mobile Data Terminal): A handheld or in-vehicle computer system providing real-time access to vehicle records, driver information, incident reporting, and communication tools.

PIT (Precision Immobilisation Technique): A maneuver used to safely stop a fleeing vehicle by causing it to spin to a controlled stop. Performed at speeds regulated by the state to minimise risk.

ROLLING BLOCK: Tactical positioning used during vehicle pursuits to contain and control a fleeing vehicle by positioning patrol units to limit movement and facilitate apprehension.

BOX: A tactical strategy using multiple patrol units positioned in a formation around the suspect vehicle, creating physical barriers on all sides to restrict escape routes and force a stop.

§2 Role Responsibilities ▾

Highway Patrol Officers: Responsible for leading pursuits, patrolling, enforcing traffic laws, and responding to incidents. Duties include traffic stops, issuing citations, investigating accidents, and addressing emergencies in accordance with SOPs.

Commander: Oversees the Highway Patrol Division, managing operations and strategic planning. Ensures alignment with departmental goals, coordinates with agencies, and provides guidance to supervisors and officers.

Superintendent: Oversees multiple units, ensuring operational objectives are met and providing strategic oversight. Coordinates with the Commander and conducts high-level reviews of procedures.

Chief Inspector: Manages divisions and ensures long-term strategies are met. Leads major incidents, conducts audits, and ensures departmental functions operate efficiently.

Deputy Chief / Chief: Leads the department and sets its vision and strategic direction. Oversees all operational divisions and administrative functions, ensuring strategic goals are implemented across the board.

§3 Chain of Command ▾

All personnel are required to adhere to the established chain of command. This involves reporting directly to their immediate supervisor and following instructions from higher-ranking officers at all times.

§4 Operational Procedures ▾

4.1 Daily Operations: Officers ensure vehicles are operational during each shift and that all equipment is functional. If damaged during shift, officers will return to station for repair or replacement.

4.2 Shift Protocols: Officers must log in at the beginning of each shift, report any issues or incidents during the shift, and log out at the end.

4.3 Routine Stops: Officers must follow proper procedures when conducting traffic stops, including approaching vehicles safely and documenting interactions via the MDT where necessary.

4.4 Traffic Enforcement: Officers are responsible for identifying and apprehending drivers hooning, speeding, or violating traffic laws following established protocols.

4.5 Accident Response: Upon arrival at an accident scene, officers must secure the area, provide aid to any injured parties, and coordinate with emergency services. Officers must collect evidence, interview witnesses, and complete an incident report. If necessary, coordinate with CID investigators.

§5 Pursuit Procedures ▾

5.1 Initiating Pursuit: Pursuits may only be initiated when there is reasonable suspicion of a serious crime or if the suspect poses a danger to the public.

5.2 Conducting Pursuit: Officers must maintain communication with each other, follow the suspect at a safe distance, and remain spaced apart — preferably in a Zipper formation.

5.3 Terminating Pursuit: Pursuits may be terminated if the risk to public safety outweighs the benefit of apprehension, or if ordered by a supervisor.

5.4 PIT Maneuvers: PIT is to be performed under 180 km/h and only when safe to do so. The default timer is 5 minutes from the beginning of pursuit. This timer can be reduced based on the actions of the suspect, including excessive civilian collisions or purposeful collisions with police vehicles.

5.5 Spike Strips: Can be used to deflate an evading vehicle's tyres. Must be used when safe to do so and must be collected before leaving the scene.

5.6 Rolling Block: Initiated during pursuit to slow or divert a fleeing vehicle. Performed by placing your vehicle in front of or beside the suspect vehicle. Gradual contact is permitted to coerce the suspect vehicle in the desired direction — this does not mean slamming brakes.

5.7 Box: A more advanced version of the rolling block, initiated with the cooperation of multiple police vehicles. Performed by positioning units around the suspect vehicle to limit mobility. May be performed while mobile but is also a primary tool for ending pursuits while stationary.

5.8 Foot Pursuits: Once a suspect has been boxed, they may attempt to flee on foot. Verbal warnings before taser deployment are preferred — this is to be implemented at the discretion of officers based on the suspect's behaviour during the pursuit.

5.9 Roadblocks: When establishing roadblocks, officers must ensure they are clearly visible to motorists with adequate warning. Proper placement and visibility are crucial for preventing accidents and ensuring drivers can safely stop.

§6 Emergency Response ▾

6.1 Major Incidents: Highway Officers must respond to incidents such as robberies, conflicts, drug crimes, or heists in coordination with other law enforcement.

6.2 Pursuit Leadership: Highway Patrol Officers engage as the primary unit in all pursuit responses.

6.3 Initiating Pursuits: Officers initiate pursuits based on reasonable suspicion of serious offences or when public safety is at risk.

6.4 Activating POLAIR: When necessary, the highest-ranking officer (per department) has the authority to activate POLAIR (Police Air Support) to assist in tracking and managing pursuits.

6.5 Direction of Travel: Officers must quickly acquire and communicate the suspect's direction of travel to all responding units, ensuring all officers are informed and can strategise accordingly.

6.6 Coordinating Tactics: As the primary unit, officers should communicate clearly with other units, coordinating tactics to manage the pursuit effectively and ensure public safety.

6.7 Managing Resources: Officers are responsible for managing available resources during pursuits, including deploying support units and monitoring traffic conditions.

6.8 Decision Making: Officers must make quick decisions on whether to continue or terminate pursuits, balancing the necessity of apprehending the suspect against potential risks to public safety.

§7 Communication Protocols ▾

7.1 Radio Communication: Officers are required to use clear and concise language when communicating over the radio, adhering to established procedures so messages are easily understood and transmitted efficiently.

7.2 Coordination: All communication with external agencies and departments must be coordinated through radio or phone to ensure information is relayed accurately and inter-agency operations are managed effectively.

7.3 Pursuit Lead: The pursuit lead is required at all times to communicate the position of the suspect vehicle and direction of travel through verbal radio communication. Simple and effective is preferred — direction followed by description and/or landmark. Overly detailed communication can become confusing under pressure.

§8 Safety Protocols & Use of Force ▾

8.1 Personal Protection: Officers are required to wear body armour during all situations to ensure their safety in hazardous environments.

8.2 Traffic Stops: Officers must approach vehicles from a safe angle, relay their position over radio, and identify the vehicle colour and type. Remain vigilant for any signs of threat and request backup if needed.

8.3 Escalation of Force: The safety of everyone involved is ALWAYS the top priority — this includes officers, the public, and the suspect(s). The level of force required will vary based on the suspect's actions. The goal is always to apprehend the suspect as unharmed as possible. The lead officer sets the force guideline; each officer is personally responsible for any actions taken outside those guidelines.

§9 Training & Qualifications ▾

9.1 Initial Training: New officers are required to complete a comprehensive training program upon joining the Highway Patrol. This covers traffic law enforcement, pursuit driving techniques, and emergency response protocols to ensure recruits are equipped with the skills needed to perform their duties safely and effectively.

9.2 Refresher Courses: To maintain up-to-date knowledge and skills, all officers must participate in refresher courses covering relevant procedures, protocol updates, and changes in traffic laws.

9.3 Pursuit Driving: Officers must complete various pursuit driving courses designed to enhance driving skills during high-speed pursuits and demonstrate understanding of all defensive driving techniques used by Highway Patrol, including PIT, rolling block, box, and spike strip deployment.

Constitutional Rights
Miranda Rights
5th & 6th Amendment Protections  ·  Custodial Interrogation Protocol
MANDATORY PROCEDURE

Miranda Rights are constitutional protections that must be read to individuals in police custody before interrogation. Derived from the Fifth and Sixth Amendments, they ensure individuals are aware of their legal protections during custodial questioning.

Failure to properly administer Miranda Rights can result in evidence being excluded from court proceedings. All SAHP personnel must be thoroughly familiar with the proper administration of these rights.

FULL VERSION
Alright, listen up. You have the right to remain silent — anything you say can and will be used against you in a court of law. You have the right to speak with an attorney and to have that attorney present during questioning. If you cannot afford an attorney, one will be provided for you by the state. Do you understand the rights I've just read to you? With these rights in mind, do you wish to speak to me now?
QUICK VERSION

You have the right to remain silent. Anything you say can be used against you in court. You have the right to an attorney. If you cannot afford one, one will be provided. Do you understand these rights?

⚖ WHEN TO ADMINISTER

Miranda Rights must be administered when:

  • The individual is in custody (not free to leave)
  • The individual is being interrogated or questioned
  • The questioning is likely to elicit an incriminating response

Failure to provide Miranda warnings when required may result in statements being inadmissible in court.

📋 PROPER ADMINISTRATION
  1. Ensure the individual is in custody.
  2. Clearly state each right using the approved wording.
  3. Confirm understanding: "Do you understand these rights?"
  4. Obtain voluntary waiver before proceeding with questioning.
  5. Document the administration in your report.

If the individual invokes their right to remain silent or requests an attorney, questioning must cease immediately.

You agree to use any licensed firearm solely for lawful purposes, including self-defense, recreational shooting, or lawful hunting within designated areas. RULE 02 Concealed Carry Regulations
You are authorized to carry a concealed firearm on your person within San Andreas where permitted by law. The firearm must remain holstered and concealed at all times, drawn only in cases of imminent self-defense, defense of others, or at approved shooting ranges.

Brandishing, displaying, or threatening others with a firearm in public without justifiable cause is strictly prohibited. This includes aggressive gestures, pointing a weapon at individuals during disputes, or any action that reasonably causes fear. Violation constitutes a misdemeanor or felony offense, immediate license revocation, and potential arrest.
RULE 04 Duty to Disclose to Law Enforcement
If stopped, questioned, or detained by any law enforcement officer: immediately inform the officer you are legally carrying a firearm. Keep hands visible and follow all lawful commands. Failure to disclose may result in criminal charges including obstruction and unlawful carry.
RULE 05 Safe Storage & Accountability
You are personally responsible for secure storage of all licensed firearms. Weapons must be stored in a locked container or with a trigger lock when not in use. If a firearm is lost, stolen, or compromised, report it to the SAHP within 24 hours or face fines, suspension, or revocation.
RULE 06 Mental & Physical Fitness to Carry
Do not possess or carry a firearm while under the influence of alcohol, illegal drugs, or impairing prescription medication; or while suffering from a mental health crisis rendering you a danger to yourself or others. The SAHP reserves the right to suspend your license pending a fitness evaluation.
RULE 07 No Unauthorized Transfer
You may not sell, gift, loan, or transfer your licensed firearm to any individual who does not hold a valid firearms license issued by the State of San Andreas. Unauthorized transfer is a Class C Felony and will result in prosecution, revocation, and permanent disqualification from future licensing.
RULE 08 Subject to Review & Revocation
Your firearms license is not permanent and remains subject to periodic review by the SAHP. The SAHP may suspend or revoke your license at any time if you violate state or county law, engage in dangerous or reckless behavior, or become ineligible due to criminal, medical, or psychological factors.

Advanced Pursuit Training
PIT Maneuver Training
Precision Immobilization Technique  ·  Sergeant Approval Required
CERTIFIED PERSONNEL ONLY

The Precision Immobilization Technique (PIT) is a high-risk vehicle intervention tactic used to safely terminate high-speed pursuits by forcing a fleeing vehicle to lose control and stop. This maneuver requires extensive training, proper judgment, and strict adherence to department policy.

Only officers who have completed the Advanced Pursuit Training course and received certification from a qualified instructor are authorized to perform PIT maneuvers. All PIT maneuvers require prior approval from a Sergeant or higher-ranking officer.

STEP-BY-STEP PROCEDURE
STEP 01
POSITIONING

Position your patrol vehicle parallel to the rear quarter of the fleeing vehicle, maintaining 2–3 feet distance. Your front bumper should align with the suspect vehicle's rear door.

  • Maintain equal or greater speed
  • Ensure a clear escape route
  • Verify no civilians in immediate vicinity
  • Communicate intention to supervisor
STEP 02
CONTACT

Once positioned, gently turn your steering wheel toward the fleeing vehicle to make contact with its rear quarter panel.

  • Front bumper to their rear quarter panel
  • Apply steady, controlled pressure
  • Do not jerk the wheel or oversteer
  • Maintain your lane position

Warning: Excessive force can cause loss of control of both vehicles.

STEP 03
TURN

As contact is maintained, smoothly turn your steering wheel in the direction of the fleeing vehicle to transfer momentum and induce oversteer.

  • Apply gentle acceleration to maintain contact pressure
  • Cause suspect vehicle's rear tires to lose traction
  • Complete maneuver within 1–2 seconds of contact
STEP 04
RELEASE & SECURE

Once the suspect vehicle begins to turn, immediately release contact and counter-steer to maintain control of your patrol vehicle.

  • Brake smoothly to a controlled stop
  • Do not continue to push the suspect vehicle
  • Position vehicle to block suspect's path if necessary
  • Call for backup and medical assistance if needed
